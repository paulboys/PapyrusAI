## Translation

**1** In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God.
**2** He [the Word] was in the beginning with God.
**3** All things came into being through him, and without him not one thing came into being.

## Notes

- **Ambiguous Words:**
  - "Word" (Λόγος): The term can be rendered as "word," but "logos" is often used in philosophical contexts to imply a deeper concept of divine reason or revelation.
- **Tense/Aspect Choices:**
  - In verse 1, the present tense ("ἦν") for both instances of "was" emphasizes ongoing existence and timeless nature. The perfect tense for "The Word was God" (Θεὸς ἦν ὁ Λόγος) is used to convey a past action with continuing relevance.
- **Key Terms:**
  - "Logos" (Word): This term carries significant theological weight, often referring to the divine reason or revelation. The rendering chosen here aims for clarity and consistency.

## Translation

**1** In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and God was the Word.
**2** This one was in the beginning with God.
**3** All things were made through him, and without him nothing came to be. He is the source of all things.

## Notes

- The Greek phrase "ἐν ἀρχῇ ἦν ὁ Λόγος" has been translated as "In the beginning was the Word," which captures the idea that God's revelation began with Jesus Christ, who is identified as the Word (John 1:14). However, some translators have opted for a more literal translation of "In the beginning was [the] Word" to emphasize the pre-existence of the Logos.
- The verb "ἦν" (eimi) can be translated as either "was" or "to be," depending on the context. In verse 1, it implies a temporal relationship ("in the beginning"), while in verses 2 and 3, it emphasizes the eternal existence of God's Word.
- The phrase "πάντα δι' αὐτοῦ ἐγένετο" (all things through him were made) suggests that Jesus Christ is the ultimate cause and source of all creation. This understanding is consistent with the broader theological context of John's Gospel.
